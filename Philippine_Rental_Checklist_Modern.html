Before the Lease: Legal Basis & Readiness

Know the Law: RA 9653 (Rent Control Act of 2009)

💡 Legal Basis: RA 9653 governs residential leases in the Philippines. Extended by RA 11203. Covers properties with monthly rent up to PHP 100,000.
Owner has proven legal ownership (title, tax declaration, or deed of absolute sale)
Property has valid Tax Declaration from Municipal/City Assessor
For condos: Certificate of Title (CCT) available and not encumbered
No pending legal disputes, mortgage, or lien — get Certified True Copy of Title
Owner has valid government-issued ID for identity verification
If managed by agent: written authorization and DRP card verified

Property Readiness Check

Water, electricity, gas connections active and transferred to owner
Unit/house is clean and in livable condition per RA 9653
All appliances tested and working (or noted as-is in writing)
Keys, access cards, remotes, parking passes accounted for
Previous tenant fully vacated and surrendered possession
Meets habitability standards: plumbing, structural integrity, ventilation
🚩 RED FLAG

If the owner cannot produce a title or refuses to show proof of ownership — do NOT proceed. This is the #1 sign of a scam.

Document everything to avoid deposit disputes

💡 Why It Matters: A thorough move-in inspection protects BOTH parties. Without documented evidence, disputes over security deposit deductions become impossible to resolve.

Tenant Screening & Essential Lease Clauses

10 checks most landlords skip

Tenant Screening Checklist

Government-issued ID (2 valid IDs)
Employment/business verification letter
Income to rent ratio (3x rule)
Credit bureau check (CIC)
Previous landlord reference
Background check: criminal records
Online presence & social media review
Co-maker or guarantor arrangement
Intention to use (residential vs commercial)
Proof of billing address

📝 Essential Clauses Every Philippine Lease Must Have

Full names, addresses, valid IDs of ALL parties (owner AND tenant)
Complete property address with specific unit/house number
Exact lease duration: start date AND end date
Monthly rent in figures AND words (e.g., PHP 18,000 — Eighteen Thousand Pesos)
Mode of payment: bank transfer details or GCash
Due date: specific day of month (e.g., 5th)
Security deposit: max 1 month rent per RA 9653
Advance rental (typically 1 month) — non-refundable
Purpose of lease: residential only
Occupancy limit: max persons allowed
Quiet enjoyment clause — tenant right to peaceful possession
Subleasing clause: prohibited or requires written consent
Termination clause: grounds and notice period
Penalty clause: e.g., 5% interest per month on overdue rent
Force majeure clause: floods, earthquakes, pandemics
Governing law: laws of the Republic of the Philippines
Signatures of witnesses and notarization (strongly recommended)

📌 Notarization Tip

A notarized lease carries more legal weight and is admissible in court. Get your lease notarized — it costs small but protects a lot.

Security Deposit: Rules, Limits & Documentation

RA 9653 Rule: Maximum 1 month rent

⚖️ Legal Limit: The security deposit shall NOT exceed one (1) month rent for residential properties. Any provision requiring more than this is void and unenforceable.
Deposit amount clearly stated in lease (maximum 1 month rent)
Purpose stated: security against unpaid rent, damages, violations
Separate bank account: owner must place deposit in interest-bearing account
Official receipt issued upon payment of deposit
Condition documented with photos + signed Move-In Report
Written inventory of all items included (furniture, appliances, fixtures)
Return clause: deposit returned within 30 days after move-out
Itemized deduction list provided if any amount withheld
Tenant forwarding address provided for refund delivery
⚠️ Important

The deposit is NOT rent. It must be kept separate by the owner and returned in full at the end of the lease, minus lawful deductions.

Utilities & Common Areas: Who Pays What

Clear lines prevent disputes

💡 Utility Responsibility Checklist

Electricity: Tenant pays from move-in date (meter reading recorded)
Water: Tenant pays from move-in date
Gas/LPG: Tenant pays; note initial tank condition
Internet/DSL/Cable: Tenant arranges and pays directly
HOA dues: Clarify if included in rent or paid separately
Meralco installation/connection fees: Who is responsible
Prepaid vs. postpaid meter: Confirm type and expectations

🏢 Common Area Responsibilities

Garbage collection: Who arranges and pays
Parking: Designated slot clearly stated; extra slots paid separately
Elevator maintenance: Covered by HOA
Hallway cleaning: Covered by HOA or building management
Pest control: Who is responsible
Building insurance: Owner covers structural; tenant covers contents
⚠️ Warning

Do not allow tenant to make unauthorized modifications to utility connections. This can cause disputes and safety hazards.

Maintenance & Repairs: Responsibilities

Civil Code Article 1654

👤 Landlord Responsibilities

Structural repairs: walls, ceiling, roof, flooring
Plumbing: major leaks, sewer, water heater
Electrical: main wiring, breakers, outlets
Aircon: major repair due to normal wear
Pest infestation (pre-existing)
Paint at start of new lease
Security issues: locks, windows, gates

🔑 Tenant Responsibilities

Minor repairs: leaky faucets (under PHP 1,000)
Replacement: light bulbs, fuses, batteries
Damage caused by tenant, guests, pets
Cleaning and general upkeep
Aircon maintenance (every 3-6 months)
Blocked drains from misuse
Report repairs within 24-48 hours

✅ Golden Rule

Normal wear and tear is NOT tenant damage. A 2-year-old carpet showing foot traffic = normal wear. A hole in the wall from removing tape = tenant damage. Document everything at move-in!

Lease Renewal & Termination: Notice Requirements

Know Your Deadlines

⏰ Before Lease Expires — Actions to Take

Notify tenant 90 days before of intention to renew or not renew (per RA 9653 IRR)
If raising rent: give 90 days written notice
Joint inspection 30 days before move-out
Review security deposit and prepare deduction list
Give tenant time to cure any lease violations

⚖️ Termination Clauses and Grounds

Non-payment of rent for 30 days after due date
Violation of lease terms: unauthorized occupants, pets, subleasing
Major damage caused by tenant or guests
Owner needs property for personal use (requires notice)
Illegal activities conducted on premises

📅 Holdover Tenant Rules

If tenant remains after lease expiry, they become month-to-month tenant
30 days notice required from either party
Cannot simply change locks — must follow legal eviction
🚫 Legal Eviction Only

No matter how justified, never attempt self-help eviction (changing locks, cutting utilities, removing belongings). Always go through the courts.

Move-Out & Security Deposit Refund

The Right Process

📦 Move-Out Procedures Checklist

Joint move-out inspection with owner/agent AND tenant present
Compare condition against Move-In Report (Section 2)
Photograph and document all areas — both keep copies
All keys, access cards, remotes returned to owner
Final meter readings recorded (electricity, water)
Outstanding utility bills settled before move-out
Rent owed for final month settled
No items belonging to tenant left in property
Tenant forwarding address provided in writing

💰 Security Deposit Refund Process

Calculate deposit: starting amount less lawful deductions
Prepare itemized list of all deductions with cost estimates
Deductions must be for: unpaid rent, damages beyond normal wear
Normal wear and tear CANNOT be deducted
Refund within 30 days from move-out date (per RA 9653)
Use traceable payment: bank transfer preferred
Obtain signed acknowledgment when refund delivered

📊 Normal Wear vs. Tenant Damage

Faded paint = normal wear (owner pays). Holes in wall = tenant damage (deduct). Mold from poor ventilation = structural issue (owner pays). The Move-In Report is your best evidence!
